Theory

Background

The physics and the algorithms that LindbladTTN puts together.

1. Open quantum systems and the density matrix

A pure quantum state $|\psi\rangle$ is a vector in a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ of dimension $d = 2^N$ for $N$ qubits. When the system is coupled to an environment (a bath, a measurement apparatus, a decoherence channel), the state is no longer pure — it must be described by a density matrix:

$$\rho = \sum_k p_k\,|\psi_k\rangle\langle\psi_k|, \qquad \rho \in \mathbb{C}^{d\times d}, \quad \rho = \rho^\dagger, \quad \mathrm{Tr}\,\rho = 1, \quad \rho \ge 0.$$

Expectation values of observables are computed as $\langle O \rangle = \mathrm{Tr}(O\rho)$.

2. The Lindblad master equation

Under the Born–Markov and rotating-wave approximations, the dynamics of $\rho$ are governed by the Gorini–Kossakowski–Sudarshan–Lindblad (GKSL) master equation:

$$ \frac{d\rho}{dt} = -\frac{i}{\hbar}\,[H(t),\,\rho] + \sum_{k} \gamma_k\!\left(L_k\,\rho\,L_k^\dagger - \tfrac{1}{2}\{L_k^\dagger L_k,\,\rho\}\right). $$

The first term is unitary evolution under the Hamiltonian $H(t)$. The sum encodes dissipation: each jump operator $L_k$ with rate $\gamma_k$ represents an irreversible channel (e.g. $L = \sigma_-$ for spontaneous emission, $L = \sigma_z$ for pure dephasing).

The GKSL form is the most general Markovian generator that preserves trace, hermiticity, and positivity of $\rho$ — i.e. it preserves physicality.

3. Vectorisation to Liouville space

Working with a matrix equation is awkward. By stacking the columns of $\rho$ into a vector $|\rho\rangle\!\rangle$, the master equation becomes a linear ODE:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\,|\rho\rangle\!\rangle = \mathcal{L}(t)\,|\rho\rangle\!\rangle$$

where $\mathcal{L}$ is the Liouvillian superoperator, a matrix of size $d^2 \times d^2$. The commutator and the dissipator both lift to $\mathcal{L}$ via

$$ -i[H,\rho]\;\longleftrightarrow\; -i\,(H\otimes I - I\otimes H^T)\,|\rho\rangle\!\rangle, \qquad L\rho L^\dagger \;\longleftrightarrow\; (L\otimes L^*)\,|\rho\rangle\!\rangle. $$

LindbladTTN uses an interleaved per-qubit vectorisation: indices for qubit $s$ are $(i_s, j_s)$ for ket/bra, kept adjacent. This makes per-site operations clean and SoP-friendly.

4. Pauli decomposition of the Liouvillian

Every $d\times d$ operator on $N$ qubits expands in the Pauli basis. LindbladTTN decomposes $H$ and each $L_k$ this way, then lifts each Pauli string into the Liouville superoperator:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_k c_k \bigotimes_{s=1}^{N} O_s^{(k)}, \qquad O_s^{(k)} \in \mathbb{C}^{4\times 4}.$$

This is a Sum-of-Products (SoP) representation: a list of terms, each a tensor product of local 4×4 superoperators (one per qubit). It avoids ever materialising the full $4^N \times 4^N$ Liouvillian.

5. Tree Tensor Networks

A Tree Tensor Network represents the vectorised density matrix $|\rho\rangle\!\rangle \in \mathbb{C}^{4^N}$ as a tree of low-rank tensors:

$$|\rho\rangle\!\rangle = \mathrm{Tr}_{\text{bonds}} \prod_{\text{nodes}} T_n,$$

where each node tensor $T_n$ has axes for its physical degrees of freedom (some 4-dimensional Liouville indices) and for its bonds to other nodes. The maximum size of a bond is the bond dimension $\chi$. Two topologies are supported:

  • Train (MPS-like) — qubits arranged in a chain. Optimal for nearest-neighbour interactions in 1D.
  • Balanced binary tree — log-depth tree of nodes. Reduces the diameter of the network, better for problems with many long-range couplings.

Crucially, the cost of operations on a TTN scales polynomially in $\chi$, not exponentially in $N$. The bond dimension acts as a single knob between cost and accuracy.

6. TDVP: Time-Dependent Variational Principle

To evolve a TTN we project the exact derivative $\mathcal{L}|\rho\rangle\!\rangle$ onto the tangent space of the manifold of rank-$\chi$ TTNs at the current point. This is the Dirac–Frenkel TDVP:

$$\frac{d|\rho\rangle\!\rangle}{dt} = P_{T_{\rho}\mathcal{M}}\,\mathcal{L}\,|\rho\rangle\!\rangle.$$

LindbladTTN implements two TDVP variants:

  • PS1 — Projector Splitting (1-site). A second-order Strang-split sweep through the tree: at each node, evolve forward by $dt/2$ under the local effective Liouvillian, move the gauge centre, evolve back along the bond by $-dt/2$, repeat. Fixed bond dimension; very stable.
  • VMF — Variable Mean-Field. The whole-network equations of motion are formed and integrated as a single ODE with torchdiffeq. Allows adaptive step sizes (e.g. dopri5) and is more flexible at the price of higher per-step cost.

7. Gauge centre and orthogonalisation

A TTN has a gauge freedom: a node and one of its neighbours can absorb an invertible matrix and its inverse without changing the represented state. PS1 exploits this by maintaining a gauge centre: at any instant, one node holds the non-orthogonal part of the state while every other node is orthogonal in a particular sense. QR moves shift the centre as the sweep advances.
